Unit 7 · c. 1900–1945

WORLD WAR I

MAIN CAUSES · TRENCH WARFARE · TOTAL WAR · VERSAILLES

Primary Source · August 1914
"The lamps are going out all over Europe; we shall not see them lit again in our lifetime."
— Sir Edward Grey, British Foreign Secretary, August 3, 1914
Causes — MAIN
MILITARISM · ALLIANCES · IMPERIALISM · NATIONALISM
THE MAIN FRAMEWORK
Militarism
European arms race — Germany's navy challenged Britain; war plans made mobilisation automatic
Alliances
Triple Alliance vs. Triple Entente — local Balkan crisis becomes world war
AP Weight
Very High — MAIN is core AP content on every exam

Militarism: European powers were in an arms race for decades. Germany's Schlieffen Plan made mobilisation automatic once war was declared — armies moved before diplomats could react.

Alliances: When Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia, Russia mobilised, triggering Germany to declare war on Russia, then France, then invade Belgium — pulling Britain in. The alliance system converted a regional dispute into a world war in six weeks.

Imperialism: Colonial competition created accumulated resentments — the Morocco Crises (1905, 1911) nearly sparked war before 1914.

Nationalism: The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand by Gavrilo Princip (Bosnian Serb nationalist, June 28, 1914) was the immediate trigger. Pan-Slavism, Pan-Germanism, and subject peoples within Austria-Hungary all created instability.

Schlieffen PlanTriple AllianceTriple EntenteFranz Ferdinand
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1914–1918 · Western Front
TRENCH WARFARE & TOTAL WAR
New Technology
Machine guns, poison gas (Ypres 1915), tanks, airplanes, submarines
Casualties
~20 million dead; Battle of the Somme: 57,000 British killed on day one
AP Weight
High — technology, total war mobilisation, propaganda

The Western Front became a 400-mile stalemate of trenches across France and Belgium. Machine guns made offensive advances suicidal. New weapons: poison gas, tanks, aerial bombing, submarine warfare. The war killed 20 million — mostly young men sent into industrialised slaughter.

Total War: Entire economies and populations mobilised. Women entered munitions factories. Governments used propaganda, rationing, censorship. Colonial troops from India, Africa, and the Caribbean were conscripted to fight in European wars they had no stake in.

⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1917 · Russia
RUSSIAN REVOLUTION
Feb 1917
Tsar overthrown; Provisional Government formed — but continues the war
Oct 1917
Lenin's Bolsheviks seize power on "Peace, Land, Bread"
AP Weight
Very High — WWI's most consequential political outcome

Russia's catastrophic losses (2 million dead by 1917) and economic collapse toppled the tsar. The Provisional Government's fatal error: continuing WWI. Lenin's Bolsheviks offered the only alternative — immediate peace, land redistribution, workers' control.

Treaty of Brest-Litovsk (1918) pulled Russia from WWI, ceding huge territory. Civil War 1918–21. Soviet Union established 1922 — the world's first communist state, whose existence shaped 20th-century geopolitics entirely.

⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1919 · Paris
TREATY OF VERSAILLES
War Guilt
Article 231 — Germany accepts sole blame for the war
Reparations
132 billion gold marks — caused hyperinflation and economic collapse
AP Weight
Very High — directly plants the seeds of WWII

Versailles punished Germany harshly: War Guilt Clause (Article 231), crushing reparations, loss of 13% of territory and all colonies, army capped at 100,000. Wilson's 14 Points (self-determination, League of Nations) were largely ignored.

German economic humiliation from Versailles + hyperinflation (1923) + Great Depression (1929) = the political conditions Hitler exploited. Every one of Hitler's early speeches invoked Versailles. Without Versailles there is no Hitler.

Unit 7 · c. 1900–1945

THE INTERWAR PERIOD

GREAT DEPRESSION · FASCISM · COMMUNISM · ANTI-COLONIAL MOVEMENTS

Primary Source · Fascism
"Fascism denies that the majority, by the simple fact that it is a majority, can direct human society."
— Benito Mussolini, "The Doctrine of Fascism," 1932
Crisis and Response 1919–1939
1929–1939 · Global
THE GREAT DEPRESSION

The 1929 Wall Street Crash triggered a global economic collapse. US GDP fell 30%; unemployment hit 25%. International trade collapsed as countries raised tariffs (Smoot-Hawley). The Depression destabilised democracies, fuelling extremist movements promising economic salvation through nationalism.

US Response — New Deal (FDR): Relief, recovery, and reform. Social Security, FDIC, public works. Permanently expanded government's role in the economy.
Germany: Hyperinflation (1923) then Depression (1929) destroyed the Weimar Republic. Hitler promised economic revival — his electoral support rose with unemployment.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1922–1945 · Europe
RISE OF FASCISM

Fascism emerged from WWI's aftermath — promising national glory, strong leadership, and rejection of both capitalism and communism. Mussolini seized power in Italy (1922). Hitler rose through democratic elections before dismantling democracy itself.

Key Features: Ultranationalism, one-party rule, cult of the leader, glorification of violence, rejection of individual rights for the state. Nazism added explicit racial hierarchy and antisemitism.
AP angle: Fascism as a response to the perceived failures of democracy and the threat of communism. Understanding its popular appeal is essential for AP essays — it was not imposed by force alone.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1928–1939 · Soviet Union
STALIN'S FIVE-YEAR PLANS

Stalin's Five-Year Plans (beginning 1928) rapidly industrialised the USSR through state command of the economy — collectivising agriculture, building heavy industry. The Holodomor (1932–33) killed 3.5–7.5 million Ukrainians through engineered famine. The Great Purge (1936–38) executed ~750,000 and imprisoned millions more.

Collectivisation: Private farms abolished; peasants forced into collective farms. Grain quotas enforced even during famine — food exported while Ukrainians starved.
Outcome: USSR became an industrial power capable of defeating Germany — but at millions of lives and a totalitarian police state.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1919–1939 · Global Colonies
ANTI-COLONIAL MOVEMENTS

WWI's promise of self-determination (Wilson's 14 Points) was betrayed at Versailles. Colonial people who fought for Britain and France received nothing. This radicalised nationalist movements globally: Gandhi's non-cooperation in India, the May Fourth Movement in China, the Wafd Party in Egypt, the Korean March First Movement.

Gandhi's Salt March (1930): 240-mile walk to the sea to make salt illegally — exposed British violence to global scrutiny. Model for non-violent resistance movements worldwide.
May Fourth Movement (China, 1919): Students protested Versailles's award of German territories in China to Japan — fuelling Chinese nationalism and the founding of the Chinese Communist Party (1921).
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
Unit 7 · c. 1900–1945

KEY FIGURES

DICTATORS · REVOLUTIONARIES · RESISTORS · LEADERS

Dictators & War Leaders
1889–1945 · Germany
ADOLF HITLER
Nazi Führer
Austrian-born WWI veteran who leveraged German humiliation and Versailles resentment to become Chancellor (1933) then Führer. Mein Kampf outlined his ideology: racial hierarchy, antisemitism, and Lebensraum. Responsible for the Holocaust and WWII — history's defining perpetrator of genocide.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1878–1953 · Soviet Union
JOSEPH STALIN
Soviet General Secretary
Rose after Lenin's death (1924). Consolidated totalitarian control via the Great Purge (~750,000 executed; millions in Gulags). Five-Year Plans industrialised the USSR at catastrophic cost. Holodomor killed millions. Signed Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact with Hitler (1939), then pivoted to the Allies after Germany invaded in 1941. The USSR bore the largest share of WWII's cost — ~27 million dead.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1883–1945 · Italy
BENITO MUSSOLINI
Fascist Duce — Italy
Founded fascism; seized power 1922 via the March on Rome. Invaded Ethiopia (1935) — ignored by the League of Nations, proving its impotence. Allied with Hitler (Rome-Berlin Axis, 1936). His rise was the template Hitler studied closely.
⚡ APMED FREQ
1870–1924 · Russia
VLADIMIR LENIN
Bolshevik Leader
Led the October Revolution (1917) on "Peace, Land, Bread." Created the Soviet Union (1922). NEP (New Economic Policy) introduced limited capitalism to stabilise the economy. His death triggered a power struggle won by Stalin. Marxism-Leninism — the vanguard party leading the proletariat — became the ideology of communist revolutions worldwide.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
Resistors & Reform Leaders
1869–1948 · India
MOHANDAS GANDHI
Anti-Colonial Leader
Developed satyagraha (non-violent resistance). Salt March (1930): 240-mile walk to defy British salt monopoly, exposing colonial violence globally. Non-cooperation boycotted British goods. His methods became the model for the US Civil Rights Movement, anti-apartheid movements, and independence movements worldwide. Assassinated 1948.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1882–1945 · USA
FRANKLIN D. ROOSEVELT
US President
New Deal (1933) used government intervention to fight the Depression — permanently expanding the federal government's role. Led the US into WWII after Pearl Harbor (1941). Yalta Conference (1945) with Churchill and Stalin shaped the postwar world and planted seeds of the Cold War.
⚡ APMED FREQ
1874–1965 · Britain
WINSTON CHURCHILL
British Prime Minister
Became PM as France fell (May 1940). Refused negotiations with Hitler when others urged appeasement. "We shall fight on the beaches" — radio speeches sustained British morale through the Blitz. Instrumental in building the Allied coalition. His imperialist positions on India and the Bengal famine (1943, ~3M dead) remain deeply contested.
⚡ APMED FREQ
1893–1976 · China
MAO ZEDONG
Chinese Communist Leader
Led the CCP through the Long March (1934–35) — a 6,000-mile strategic retreat that became Communist mythology. Allied with Nationalists against Japan during WWII. Won the Chinese Civil War in 1949 — People's Republic of China established. The world's most populous communist state.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
Unit 7 · c. 1900–1945

WORLD WAR II

CAUSES · CONDUCT · THE HOLOCAUST · CONSEQUENCES

THE DEADLIEST CONFLICT IN HUMAN HISTORY
70–85 MILLION DEAD
WWII (1939–1945) killed more people than any conflict in history — the majority of them civilians. It began with Hitler's invasion of Poland and ended with atomic bombs over Japan. Between: the Holocaust, blitzkrieg, the Pacific War, the Eastern Front (the war's true killing ground at 30+ million dead), and the birth of the nuclear age. Every subsequent decade of world history flows from what happened here.
Key Developments
CAUSES OF WWII
ROOTS IN VERSAILLES & APPEASEMENT
Versailles humiliation + Great Depression + fascism + appeasement. Munich Agreement (1938) gave Hitler the Sudetenland — Chamberlain's "peace in our time" lasted six months. Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact (1939) divided Eastern Europe. Germany invaded Poland September 1, 1939.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
MILITARY TECHNOLOGY
BLITZKRIEG & NEW WARFARE
Germany's Blitzkrieg ("lightning war") combined tanks, aircraft, and mechanised infantry — France fell in 6 weeks (1940). Battle of Britain: first major all-air campaign; RAF held. U-boats nearly starved Britain. Strategic bombing of cities (London Blitz, Dresden, Tokyo). Radar and codebreaking (Enigma/Ultra) shaped the outcome.
⚡ APMED FREQ
GENOCIDE
THE HOLOCAUST
Nazi Germany's systematic murder of ~6 million Jews and ~5 million others (Roma, disabled people, LGBTQ+, political prisoners). Wannsee Conference (1942) organised the "Final Solution." Six death camps in occupied Poland — Auschwitz-Birkenau alone killed 1.1 million. The defining atrocity of the 20th century — and a defining AP exam topic.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
PACIFIC THEATER
JAPAN'S EMPIRE & DEFEAT
Japan invaded Manchuria (1931), China (Nanjing Massacre, 200,000+ killed, 1937), and Southeast Asia. Pearl Harbor attack (December 7, 1941) brought the US into the war. Island-hopping campaign across the Pacific. Atomic bombs on Hiroshima (August 6) and Nagasaki (August 9, 1945) — only nuclear weapons used in combat — forced Japan's surrender and opened the nuclear age.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
EASTERN FRONT
OPERATION BARBAROSSA & STALINGRAD
Germany's invasion of the USSR (June 1941) — 3 million troops, the largest military operation in history. Initial successes reversed at Stalingrad (1942–43) where the German Sixth Army was destroyed. The Eastern Front killed 30+ million — this was the war's true turning point. The Soviet Union, not the Western Allies, broke the backbone of the Wehrmacht.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
CONSEQUENCES
THE POSTWAR ORDER
United Nations (1945) replaced the failed League. Nuremberg Trials established that war crimes and crimes against humanity are prosecutable — "following orders" is not a defence. Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948). Decolonisation accelerated as European powers were weakened. Cold War began immediately — the US and Soviet Union emerged as rival superpowers.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
Primary Source · Nuremberg, 1946
"To initiate a war of aggression is not only an international crime; it is the supreme international crime."
— Nuremberg Tribunal Judgment, 1946

MASS ATROCITIES

ARMENIAN GENOCIDE · HOLODOMOR · HOLOCAUST · AP FRAMEWORKS

Primary Source · Martin Niemöller, c. 1946
"First they came for the socialists, and I did not speak out — because I was not a socialist..."
— Martin Niemöller, German Lutheran pastor who survived Dachau
Defining Atrocities of the Era
1915–1923 · Ottoman Empire
ARMENIAN GENOCIDE

The Ottoman government's systematic extermination of ~1–1.5 million Armenians during WWI. The Young Turk government used wartime emergency powers to order mass deportations and massacres of a Christian minority it blamed for military setbacks. Death marches through the Syrian desert killed hundreds of thousands.

The Armenian Genocide is recognised as the 20th century's first modern genocide — and a direct precursor to the Holocaust. Hitler reportedly asked before invading Poland: "Who today remembers the Armenians?" — showing how past impunity enables future genocide. Denied by Turkey to this day.

Young TurksDeath MarchesState-Organised
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1932–1933 · Soviet Ukraine
THE HOLODOMOR

Stalin's collectivisation policies caused a catastrophic famine in Soviet Ukraine killing an estimated 3.5–7.5 million people. The Soviet government continued exporting grain from Ukraine even as people starved to death in the streets of Kyiv. Evidence shows the famine was deliberately intensified to break Ukrainian resistance to collectivisation and crush Ukrainian national identity.

The Holodomor (Ukrainian: "death by starvation") is recognised as a genocide by Ukraine and 30+ countries, though Russia still disputes this. It demonstrates how totalitarian states weaponised food as a tool of mass murder — a pattern that recurred in Mao's China (Great Leap Forward) and North Korea.

⚡ APMED FREQ
1941–1945 · Nazi-Occupied Europe
THE HOLOCAUST

The Nazi regime's systematic murder of ~6 million Jews and ~5 million others. The "Final Solution" (Wannsee Conference, 1942) organised industrial-scale murder through gas chambers at six death camps: Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, Chelmno, Majdanek. Auschwitz-Birkenau alone murdered 1.1 million people.

The Holocaust required thousands of ordinary participants — bureaucrats, soldiers, train operators — demonstrating that genocide is a social process requiring mass complicity, not just a few evil leaders. Nuremberg Trials (1945–46) established international criminal law. The UN Genocide Convention (1948) defined genocide and created a legal framework for prosecution.

⚡ APHIGH FREQ
AP FRAMEWORK
PATTERNS & CAUSES OF GENOCIDE

For AP essays, understanding WHY genocides happen matters as much as WHAT happened. Common conditions: a dehumanising ideology targeting a specific group; a state apparatus willing to use systematic violence; wartime or crisis conditions reducing international scrutiny; bystander nations unwilling to intervene; and perpetrators who expect no future accountability (as Hitler noted about the Armenians).

Gregory Stanton's "Ten Stages of Genocide" framework provides a useful analytical structure: Classification → Symbolisation → Discrimination → Dehumanisation → Organisation → Polarisation → Preparation → Persecution → Extermination → Denial. Each of these stages is identifiable in the Armenian Genocide, Holodomor, and Holocaust. This pattern-recognition framework is directly applicable to AP LEQ and DBQ prompts about comparative atrocities.

CHRONOLOGICAL TIMELINE

KEY EVENTS 1900–1945 · FILTER BY CATEGORY

June 28, 1914
ASSASSINATION OF FRANZ FERDINAND ⚡ AP
Gavrilo Princip (Bosnian Serb nationalist) assassinates Archduke Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo. Austria-Hungary issues ultimatum to Serbia. Alliance cascade begins. By August all major European powers are at war.
1915
ARMENIAN GENOCIDE BEGINS ⚡ AP
Ottoman government orders deportation and massacre of Armenian minority under WWI emergency powers. ~1–1.5 million killed in death marches and massacres. The 20th century's first modern genocide — directly precedes the Holocaust.
1917
RUSSIAN REVOLUTION ⚡ AP
February: Tsar overthrown; Provisional Government formed but continues the war. October: Lenin's Bolsheviks seize power on "Peace, Land, Bread." Treaty of Brest-Litovsk (1918) pulls Russia from WWI. Soviet Union established 1922.
1919
TREATY OF VERSAILLES ⚡ AP
Germany assigned sole blame (Art. 231), 132 billion gold marks reparations, loses 13% territory. Wilson's 14 Points ignored. League of Nations created — US Senate refuses to join. German resentment becomes Hitler's most powerful political weapon.
1922
MUSSOLINI SEIZES POWER — ITALY
March on Rome — fascist Blackshirts march on the capital. King Victor Emmanuel III hands Mussolini power. First fascist state. Hitler studies Mussolini's methods closely and models the Beer Hall Putsch (1923) on the March on Rome.
1929
WALL STREET CRASH — GREAT DEPRESSION ⚡ AP
Black Thursday (Oct 24) triggers global economic collapse. US GDP falls 30%; unemployment hits 25%. The Depression destroys the Weimar Republic — Hitler's electoral support rises as unemployment does. FDR's New Deal expands US government permanently.
1919
MAY FOURTH MOVEMENT — CHINA ⚡ AP
Chinese students protest Versailles giving German territories in China to Japan. Radicalises Chinese nationalism and directly leads to the founding of the Chinese Communist Party (1921). Shows how WWI's broken promises fuelled anti-colonial movements globally.
1930
GANDHI'S SALT MARCH ⚡ AP
240-mile march to the sea to defy British salt monopoly. Massively publicised globally. British police beat unarmed marchers — exposing colonial violence. Demonstrates how non-violent resistance can be a powerful political strategy.
1932–1933
HOLODOMOR — UKRAINE ⚡ AP
Stalin's collectivisation causes deliberate famine in Ukraine — 3.5–7.5 million killed. Grain exported while people starve. Recognised as genocide by Ukraine and 30+ nations. Shows how totalitarian states weaponise food as mass murder.
1933
HITLER BECOMES CHANCELLOR ⚡ AP
Nazi Party wins 37% (1932) — largest in Reichstag. Hitler appointed Chancellor Jan 30. Reichstag Fire used to suspend civil liberties. Enabling Act (March) gives Hitler dictatorial powers. Democracy dismantled through legal mechanisms.
Sept 1, 1939
WORLD WAR II BEGINS ⚡ AP
Germany invades Poland. Britain and France declare war. Blitzkrieg — combined armour, air power, and infantry — overwhelms Polish defences in weeks. The failure of appeasement (Munich, 1938) is confirmed. WWII has begun.
1941–1945
THE HOLOCAUST ⚡ AP
Wannsee Conference (Jan 1942) organises the "Final Solution." Six death camps operational in Poland. ~6 million Jews and ~5 million others systematically murdered. Defining atrocity of the 20th century. Nuremberg Trials establish international criminal law.
Dec 7, 1941
PEARL HARBOR — US ENTERS WWII
Japan's surprise attack kills 2,403 Americans. US declares war on Japan; Germany and Italy declare war on US. America's industrial capacity enters the Allied war effort — decisively shifting the balance of production.
1942–1943
BATTLE OF STALINGRAD — TURNING POINT ⚡ AP
German Sixth Army destroyed at Stalingrad — 800,000 Axis casualties. The war's true turning point. After Stalingrad, Germany is permanently on the defensive on the Eastern Front. The Soviet Union breaks the backbone of the Nazi war machine.
Aug 6 & 9, 1945
ATOMIC BOMBS — HIROSHIMA & NAGASAKI ⚡ AP
~200,000 killed immediately. Japan surrenders September 2. WWII ends. The nuclear age begins — reshaping geopolitics, the logic of warfare, and the entire framework of the Cold War. MAD (Mutually Assured Destruction) prevents direct superpower conflict for the next 45 years.
