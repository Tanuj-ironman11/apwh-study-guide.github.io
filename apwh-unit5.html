Unit 5 · c. 1750–1900

ATLANTIC REVOLUTIONS

ENLIGHTENMENT IDEAS · INDEPENDENCE MOVEMENTS · POLITICAL TRANSFORMATION

Primary Source · Enlightenment Philosophy
"Men are born free, and everywhere they are in chains. One thinks himself the master of others, and still remains a greater slave than they."
— Jean-Jacques Rousseau, The Social Contract, 1762
The Revolutions
1775–1783 · North America
AMERICAN REVOLUTION
Cause
Taxation without representation; Enlightenment ideals
Outcome
First large-scale democracy since ancient Greece
AP Weight
High — model for subsequent revolutions

Colonists resented British taxation policies like the Stamp Act (1765) and Tea Act (1773) — seen as violations of the Enlightenment principle of government by consent. Philosophers like Locke argued governments derived legitimacy from the people.

The Declaration of Independence (1776) codified Enlightenment ideals into a founding document — influencing every subsequent revolution. The Articles of Confederation (weak) → U.S. Constitution (1787) demonstrated the difficulty of translating revolutionary ideals into stable governance.

Locke's Social Contract No Taxation w/o Representation Declaration of Independence Articles of Confederation
Global Influence
1789–1799 · France
FRENCH REVOLUTION
Cause
Economic crisis, Estate system inequality, Enlightenment
Outcome
Republic → Reign of Terror → Napoleonic Era
AP Weight
Very High — most complex revolution

France's rigid Three Estate system (clergy, nobility, commoners) left 97% of the population bearing full tax burdens. The Estates-General convocation in 1789 escalated into the storming of the Bastille and the Declaration of the Rights of Man — proclaiming liberty, equality, and sovereignty of the people.

The Reign of Terror (1793–94) under Robespierre executed ~40,000 "enemies of the Republic." Napoleon Bonaparte later seized power (1799), spreading revolutionary legal codes (Napoleonic Code) across Europe even as he undermined the revolution's democratic ideals.

Three Estates Declaration of Rights of Man Reign of Terror Napoleonic Code Liberté Égalité Fraternité
Radical Intensity
1791–1804 · Haiti (Saint-Domingue)
HAITIAN REVOLUTION
Cause
French Revolutionary ideals + brutal plantation system
Outcome
First successful enslaved-led revolution in history
AP Weight
Very High — most significant for AP essays

Haiti (Saint-Domingue) was France's most profitable colony — producing 40% of Europe's sugar and 60% of its coffee through enslaved African labor. When France proclaimed liberty and equality, enslaved Haitians asked: what about us?

Toussaint L'Ouverture led a massive uprising beginning 1791. Despite Napoleon sending troops to re-establish slavery (and the death of Toussaint in a French prison), Jean-Jacques Dessalines declared independence in 1804 — the only successful revolution led by enslaved people in history. The U.S. and European powers refused to recognize Haiti for decades — fearing it would inspire enslaved populations elsewhere.

Toussaint L'Ouverture Jean-Jacques Dessalines First Black Republic Anti-Slavery Revolution
Historical Uniqueness
1810s–1820s · Latin America
LATIN AMERICAN REVOLUTIONS
Cause
Creole resentment, Enlightenment, Napoleon's invasion of Spain
Outcome
Independence of every nation in Latin America
AP Weight
High — social hierarchy continuity key theme

Creoles (Spanish-born in Americas) resented being excluded from top colonial offices despite being European. Napoleon's occupation of Spain (1808) created a power vacuum — Creoles declared provisional governments in Spain's name, then independence.

Simón Bolívar ("El Libertador") liberated Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Bolivia. José de San Martín liberated Argentina and Chile. Key difference: Latin American revolutions largely preserved existing social hierarchies — Creoles replaced Spanish but Indigenous and enslaved populations gained little freedom.

Simón Bolívar José de San Martín Creole Elites Social Hierarchy Continuity
Social Change

"I was born a slave, but nature gave me a soul of a free man. Liberty is taken by force — it is not given."
— Attributed to Toussaint L'Ouverture, c. 1800
Unit 5 · c. 1750–1900

THE ENLIGHTENMENT

REASON · NATURAL RIGHTS · SOCIAL CONTRACT · POLITICAL PHILOSOPHY

Primary Source · The Enlightenment
"Dare to know! Have the courage to use your own understanding — that is the motto of the Enlightenment."
— Immanuel Kant, What Is Enlightenment?, 1784
Core Enlightenment Ideas
FOUNDATION · 17th–18th century
WHAT WAS THE ENLIGHTENMENT?

An 18th-century intellectual movement centered in Western Europe — especially France, Britain, and Scotland — that championed reason, science, and individual rights over tradition, superstition, and divine authority.

Break from tradition: Enlightenment thinkers used reason and empirical observation to challenge Church authority and the divine right of kings.
Scientific Revolution link: Newton's laws (1687) proved the universe followed rational laws — encouraging thinkers to apply reason to society and government.
Salons & coffeehouses: Ideas spread through informal gatherings, pamphlets, and the French Encyclopédie — the first attempt to compile all human knowledge.
Legacy: Directly inspired the American Revolution, French Revolution, and independence movements across the Atlantic world.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
CONCEPT · John Locke, 1689
NATURAL RIGHTS & SOCIAL CONTRACT

The two most AP-tested Enlightenment concepts — Locke's natural rights and social contract theory directly shaped the American Declaration of Independence and French Revolution.

Natural Rights (Locke): All humans possess inalienable rights — life, liberty, and property — that governments cannot legitimately take away.
Social Contract: Governments derive legitimacy from the consent of the governed. If a government violates the people's rights, citizens have the right to overthrow it.
Tabula Rasa: Locke argued humans are born a "blank slate" — knowledge comes from experience, not innate ideas. This challenged aristocratic claims of inherent superiority.
AP Connection: Jefferson's "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" directly adapts Locke's "life, liberty, and property."
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
CONCEPT · Montesquieu & Rousseau
SEPARATION OF POWERS & POPULAR SOVEREIGNTY

Two ideas that reshaped how governments were structured — one preventing tyranny through checks on power, the other grounding legitimacy in the people themselves.

Montesquieu — Separation of Powers: The Spirit of the Laws (1748) argued government should be divided into legislative, executive, and judicial branches to prevent any one from becoming tyrannical.
Checks & Balances: Each branch should limit the others. The U.S. Constitution's structure is directly modeled on Montesquieu's framework.
Rousseau — Popular Sovereignty: The Social Contract (1762) — "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." Governments derive legitimacy only from the General Will of the people.
Rousseau vs Locke: Locke focused on protecting individual rights; Rousseau on the collective will. French revolutionaries drew more on Rousseau; Americans on Locke.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
CONCEPT · Voltaire & Press Freedom
FREEDOM OF THOUGHT & RELIGIOUS TOLERATION

Voltaire attacked religious intolerance, censorship, and the power of the Church — arguing reason should replace superstition in all areas of life.

Voltaire on religion: "Crush the infamous thing!" — his battle cry against religious fanaticism. Argued for tolerance between religions and freedom of conscience.
Candide (1759): Satirical novel attacking religious hypocrisy, war, and optimistic philosophy — reached millions through underground circulation across Europe.
Press freedom: Enlightenment thinkers argued censorship blocked truth. The Encyclopédie (Diderot) was banned by the French Crown for undermining Church authority.
Deism: Many Enlightenment figures (including Jefferson, Franklin) were Deists — believing in a rational creator God who didn't intervene in human affairs.
CONCEPT · Adam Smith & Economics
FREE MARKETS & ECONOMIC LIBERALISM

Enlightenment thinking extended to economics — challenging mercantilism and arguing that free markets, not government control, produced the greatest prosperity.

Adam Smith — Wealth of Nations (1776): Free markets guided by an "invisible hand" allocate resources more efficiently than government control. Foundation of liberal economics.
Laissez-faire: "Let it be" — government should not interfere with commerce. Directly opposed to mercantilist policies of Unit 4.
Division of labor: Specialization increases productivity — Smith's pin factory example showed workers doing one task outproduced generalists by orders of magnitude.
AP Connection: Smith's ideas underpinned 19th century British free trade policy (repeal of Corn Laws, 1846) and justified industrial capitalism's lack of regulation.
⚡ APMED FREQ
LIMITS · Who was excluded?
CONTRADICTIONS OF THE ENLIGHTENMENT

Enlightenment ideas proclaimed universal rights — but their authors largely excluded women, enslaved people, and colonized peoples from their vision of freedom.

Women excluded: Mary Wollstonecraft's Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792) directly challenged Enlightenment thinkers who applied natural rights only to men.
Slavery contradiction: Locke invested in the Royal African Company. Jefferson wrote "all men are created equal" while enslaving 600+ people. The Haitian Revolution exposed this hypocrisy.
Colonialism: Enlightenment Europeans justified colonialism through "scientific racism" — ranking human groups hierarchically — directly contradicting universal rights claims.
AP Angle: The tension between Enlightenment ideals and their exclusions is a core AP exam theme — especially in essays about the Haitian Revolution and women's movements.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ

FRANCE · 1689–1755
MONTESQUIEU ⚡ AP
The Spirit of the Laws (1748). Separation of powers into three branches. Checks and balances. Directly shaped the U.S. Constitution's structure.
FRANCE · 1694–1778
VOLTAIRE ⚡ AP
Candide. Attacked religious intolerance, censorship, and political tyranny. Champion of free speech and separation of church and state. Corresponded with Frederick the Great and Catherine the Great.
FRANCE · 1712–1778
ROUSSEAU ⚡ AP
The Social Contract (1762). Popular sovereignty — the "General Will." "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." Influenced French Revolution more than any other thinker.
SCOTLAND · 1723–1790
ADAM SMITH ⚡ AP
Wealth of Nations (1776). Free markets, invisible hand, laissez-faire. Division of labor. Foundation of modern economics. Published the same year as American independence.
ENGLAND · 1759–1797
MARY WOLLSTONECRAFT
Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792). Applied Enlightenment logic to women — if reason is universal, women deserve equal education and rights. Mother of modern feminism. Mother of Mary Shelley.
Unit 5 · c. 1750–1900

KEY FIGURES

PHILOSOPHERS · REVOLUTIONARIES · INDUSTRIALISTS · REFORMERS

Enlightenment Philosophers
1632–1704 · England
JOHN LOCKE
Political Philosopher
Argued governments derive authority from the consent of the governed. Natural rights to life, liberty, and property. Justification for revolution if government violates those rights — foundational to the American Revolution.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1712–1778 · Geneva/France
JEAN-JACQUES ROUSSEAU
Political Philosopher
Argued for popular sovereignty — the "general will" of the people as the only legitimate basis of law. The Social Contract (1762) directly inspired the French Revolution's ideals of equality and republicanism.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1723–1790 · Scotland
ADAM SMITH
Economist
The Wealth of Nations (1776) argued for laissez-faire economics — free markets guided by an "invisible hand" without government interference. Father of modern capitalism and free trade ideology.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1743–1826 · Virginia
THOMAS JEFFERSON
Revolutionary Leader
Principal author of the Declaration of Independence. Drew directly on Locke to articulate the right to revolution against tyrannical government. First major application of Enlightenment theory to political independence.
1743–1803 · Haiti
TOUSSAINT L'OUVERTURE
Haitian Revolutionary
Born enslaved, became the military genius behind Haiti's revolution. Negotiated with France and Spain while pursuing independence. Captured and imprisoned by Napoleon but died before Haitian independence was declared (1804).
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1783–1830 · Venezuela
SIMÓN BOLÍVAR
Latin American Liberator
"El Libertador" — liberated Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Bolivia from Spanish rule. Dreamed of a unified Gran Colombia. His campaigns showed both the power and limits of revolutionary nationalism.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1818–1883 · Germany
KARL MARX
Political Economist / Socialist
The Communist Manifesto (1848) with Engels. Argued capitalism's class struggle between bourgeoisie (owners) and proletariat (workers) would inevitably lead to revolution. Inspired labor movements, unions, and socialist parties worldwide.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1815–1898 · Germany/Prussia
OTTO VON BISMARCK
Prussian Statesman
United German states into the German Empire through "blood and iron" — wars against Denmark, Austria, and France. Pioneered Realpolitik — ruthlessly practical politics over ideology. Created the world's first welfare state to undercut socialist movements.
⚡ APMED FREQ
Unit 5 · c. 1750–1900

LABOR & SOCIETY

WORKING CLASS · REFORM MOVEMENTS · GENDER ROLES · URBANIZATION

THE INDUSTRIAL SOCIAL TRANSFORMATION
THE WORLD WORKERS MADE
Industrialization didn't just change how goods were made — it fundamentally restructured society. New classes emerged, old hierarchies shifted, and movements arose to challenge the brutal conditions of early industrial capitalism. Women and children entered factories. Cities exploded in size. And the workers who built industrial wealth began to organize for their share of it.
Reactions & Movements
WORKING CLASS RESPONSE
LABOR UNIONS
Workers organized into unions to bargain collectively for wages, hours, and conditions. Combination Acts in Britain (1799) initially banned unions — later repealed (1824). By 1900 union membership was in millions. Tactics: strikes, slowdowns, collective bargaining.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
RADICAL IDEOLOGY
SOCIALISM & MARXISM
Marx and Engels argued capitalism exploited workers by extracting "surplus value" — profit taken from unpaid labor. The Communist Manifesto (1848) called workers to unite internationally. Socialist parties formed across Europe by 1880s. Revolutionary vs. reformist socialism became a key debate.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
LUDDITE MOVEMENT · 1811–1816
MACHINE BREAKING
Skilled English textile workers (Luddites) destroyed factory machinery that threatened their livelihoods. Not anti-technology per se — opposed to the use of machinery to circumvent labor practices. British government deployed military to suppress them; leaders hanged.
WOMEN'S RIGHTS
FEMINIST MOVEMENTS
Women entered industrial labor but were paid less. The Seneca Falls Convention (1848, USA) issued the Declaration of Sentiments — demanding voting rights. Mary Wollstonecraft's Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792) was the first major feminist manifesto. Suffrage movements grew through the century.
⚡ APMED FREQ
SOCIAL CLASS SHIFTS
NEW CLASS STRUCTURE
Industrial capitalism created new classes: the industrial bourgeoisie (factory owners, capitalists) and the urban proletariat (wage workers). Middle class (managers, professionals) expanded. Old aristocracy declined. Class identity replaced estate identity — reshaping politics and culture.
URBANIZATION
THE INDUSTRIAL CITY
London's population went from 1 million (1800) to 4.5 million (1880). Factory cities like Manchester became global symbols of industrial capitalism. Overcrowding, pollution, cholera, child labor, and 14-hour workdays characterized early industrial cities — driving reform movements.
Primary Source · Communist Manifesto, 1848
"The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win."
— Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, The Communist Manifesto, 1848

NATIONALISM & UNIFICATION

NATION-STATES · GERMAN UNIFICATION · ITALIAN UNIFICATION · GLOBAL EFFECTS

Primary Source · Nationalism Defined
"A nation is a soul, a spiritual principle. Two things constitute this soul: one is the possession in common of a rich legacy of memories; the other is present-day consent, the desire to live together."
— Ernest Renan, "What is a Nation?", 1882
Nationalist Movements
THE CONCEPT
WHAT IS NATIONALISM?

Nationalism is the belief that people sharing a common language, culture, religion, or ethnicity should form their own sovereign nation-state. Enlightenment ideas about popular sovereignty provided the intellectual foundation.

Nationalism was a double-edged force: it unified fragmented peoples (Germany, Italy) while it threatened to destroy multi-ethnic empires (Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian). By 1900, nationalism was the dominant ideology reshaping the political map of the world.

Popular Sovereignty Self-Determination Nation-State
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1815–1871 · Central Europe
GERMAN UNIFICATION

Germany was 39 fragmented states in 1815. Otto von Bismarck, Prussia's Chancellor, unified them through a deliberate strategy of three wars: against Denmark (1864), Austria (1866), and France (1870–71). The Franco-Prussian War's victory enabled German states to proclaim the German Empire (Reich) in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles — humiliating France.

Bismarck's method became a model: Realpolitik — using power, alliances, and calculated war rather than revolutionary idealism to achieve national goals. The new German Empire rapidly industrialized to challenge British industrial supremacy.

⚡ APHIGH FREQ
1815–1871 · Italian Peninsula
ITALIAN UNIFICATION (RISORGIMENTO)

Italy was divided into kingdoms, duchies, and Papal States. Three key figures drove unification: Count Cavour (diplomatic realpolitik, allied with France against Austria), Giuseppe Garibaldi (military conquest — his "Thousand" redshirts seized Sicily and Naples), and Giuseppe Mazzini (intellectual nationalist, "Young Italy" movement).

The Kingdom of Italy was proclaimed in 1861 (Venetia added 1866, Rome 1870). Unlike Germany, Italy unified through a combination of diplomacy, military force, and popular revolutionary movements.

19th Century · Declining Empires
NATIONALISM & EMPIRE DECLINE

Nationalism was a destructive force for multi-ethnic empires. The Ottoman Empire — "the sick man of Europe" — lost Greece (1821), Serbia, Romania, and Bulgaria to nationalist movements throughout the century. The Congress of Berlin (1878) redrew Balkan borders, accelerating Ottoman decline.

The Austro-Hungarian Empire faced constant nationalist pressure from Czechs, Slovaks, Poles, and South Slavs. These pressures would eventually trigger WWI in 1914 — showing how 19th century nationalism planted the seeds of 20th century catastrophe.

CHRONOLOGICAL TIMELINE

KEY EVENTS 1750–1900 · FILTER BY CATEGORY

1769
JAMES WATT'S STEAM ENGINE ⚡ AP
Watt patents his separate condenser improvement, making steam power efficient enough for industrial application. Triggers the factory system and the age of mechanized production.
1775–1783
AMERICAN REVOLUTION ⚡ AP
American colonists declare independence from Britain. Declaration of Independence (1776) codifies Enlightenment natural rights. First large-scale democratic republic since ancient Greece.
1776
THE WEALTH OF NATIONS — ADAM SMITH ⚡ AP
Smith publishes his foundational argument for free market capitalism and laissez-faire economics. Provides ideological justification for industrial capitalism and opposition to mercantilist regulation.
1789–1799
FRENCH REVOLUTION ⚡ AP
Overthrow of the Ancien Régime. Declaration of the Rights of Man. Reign of Terror. Rise of Napoleon. Spreads revolutionary ideals (and backlash) across Europe.
1791–1804
HAITIAN REVOLUTION ⚡ AP
Enslaved people of Saint-Domingue rise against French colonial rule. Toussaint L'Ouverture leads the military campaign. Haiti declares independence — first Black republic, only successful enslaved-led revolution in history.
1820s
STEAM LOCOMOTIVES & RAILWAYS
First commercial steam railways in Britain. By 1840s, railroads connected major industrial cities. By 1900, virtually every industrialized nation had comprehensive rail networks — transforming commerce and migration.
1810s–1820s
LATIN AMERICAN REVOLUTIONS ⚡ AP
Wave of independence movements inspired by Enlightenment ideas and Napoleon's weakening of Spain. Bolívar and San Martín liberate most of South America. Mexico, Central America independent by 1823.
1848
COMMUNIST MANIFESTO & REVOLUTIONS OF 1848 ⚡ AP
Marx and Engels publish The Communist Manifesto. Simultaneously, liberal nationalist revolutions erupt across Europe (France, Germany, Austria, Italy) — most fail, but establish the age of mass politics and ideological conflict.
1856
BESSEMER STEEL PROCESS
Henry Bessemer's process makes steel production cheap and rapid. Enables massive railway expansion, skyscrapers, warships, and bridges — launching the Second Industrial Revolution and transforming construction worldwide.
1861–1871
ITALIAN & GERMAN UNIFICATION ⚡ AP
Italy proclaimed Kingdom (1861). Germany unified into German Empire under Bismarck (1871) after defeating France. Two of Europe's most powerful nation-states created through nationalist movements — redrawing the continental balance of power.
1868
MEIJI RESTORATION — JAPAN ⚡ AP
Meiji government takes power, embarks on rapid state-directed industrialization. Model of defensive modernization — adopting Western technology while preserving Japanese culture and sovereignty. Only non-Western power to successfully industrialize in this era.
1870s–1900
SECOND INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTION
Steel, chemicals, electricity, and petroleum power a new wave of industrialization. Germany and the US surpass Britain in industrial output. New industries: automobiles, chemicals, electrical systems. Corporate capitalism replaces individual entrepreneur model.
1894–1895
FIRST SINO-JAPANESE WAR
Japan defeats China — demonstrating the success of Meiji modernization vs. Qing resistance to reform. Japan gains Taiwan and influence over Korea. Shocks Western powers: Asia has produced a modern industrial military power.
