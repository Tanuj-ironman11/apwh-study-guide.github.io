Unit 6 · c. 1760–1830

ORIGINS OF INDUSTRIALIZATION

WHY BRITAIN FIRST · PRECONDITIONS · AGRICULTURAL REVOLUTION

Primary Source · Adam Smith, 1776
"The greatest improvement in the productive powers of labor, and the greater part of the skill, dexterity, and judgment with which it is any where directed, or applied, seem to have been the effects of the division of labour."
— Adam Smith, Wealth of Nations, 1776
Why Britain First?
GEOGRAPHY · Natural Advantages
COAL, IRON & WATERWAYS

Britain's geography gave it unique advantages no other European nation possessed simultaneously — the essential raw materials for steam-powered manufacturing sat directly beside the transport networks to distribute them.

Coal deposits: Massive, shallow coal fields in Yorkshire, Lancashire, and Wales sat near navigable rivers — cheap fuel for steam engines.
Iron ore: Proximity of coal and iron ore allowed efficient steel production — the backbone of machinery and railways.
River network: Britain's rivers and 4,000 miles of canals (by 1800) transported bulk goods cheaply before railways existed.
Island advantage: No interior was more than 70 miles from navigable water — dramatically lowering transport costs for raw materials and finished goods.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
ECONOMICS · Financial Revolution
CAPITAL & CREDIT

Britain had developed sophisticated financial institutions that allowed entrepreneurs to raise capital for expensive factories — a century before France or Germany developed comparable systems.

Bank of England (1694): Stabilized credit markets and allowed long-term lending for industrial investment.
Stock markets: The London Stock Exchange allowed entrepreneurs to raise capital from many investors — spreading risk.
Low interest rates: British interest rates (3–5%) were half those in France, making factory investment far cheaper.
Profits from empire: Wealth from the Atlantic trade and colonial markets funded industrial investment.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
SOCIETY · Labor Supply
AGRICULTURAL REVOLUTION

Britain's agricultural transformation preceded industrialization — enclosures, crop rotation, and selective breeding increased food output while simultaneously driving rural workers off the land into cities.

Enclosure Acts: Parliament consolidated open fields into private farms. Millions of peasants lost common land rights → moved to cities for work.
Crop rotation: Norfolk four-course rotation (wheat, turnips, barley, clover) eliminated fallow years — producing more food per acre with fewer workers.
Urban labor pool: Displaced agricultural workers flooded cities like Manchester and Birmingham — creating a cheap industrial workforce.
⚡ APMED FREQ
EMPIRE · Colonial Markets
RAW MATERIALS & CAPTIVE MARKETS

Britain's global empire provided both the raw materials to feed factories and the markets to absorb manufactured output — a self-reinforcing engine of industrial growth unavailable to other powers.

Raw cotton from India: Indian cotton flooded British textile mills while cheap British cotton goods destroyed India's own textile industry.
Captive markets: British colonies were required to buy British manufactured goods — guaranteeing demand for factory output.
AP Key Theme: The same empire that fueled British industrialization actively prevented industrialization in India, China, and Latin America — a core AP essay topic.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
Unit 6 · Technology

KEY TECHNOLOGIES

STEAM · TEXTILES · RAILWAYS · STEEL · TELEGRAPH

The Technologies That Changed Everything
1769 · JAMES WATT
STEAM ENGINE ⚡ AP

James Watt's separate condenser (1769) made the steam engine 4x more fuel-efficient than Newcomen's original (1712). This single innovation unlocked industrial-scale power — pumping water from mines, driving textile mills, and eventually powering locomotives and steamships.

Newcomen → Watt: Newcomen's 1712 engine was too inefficient for widespread use. Watt's separate condenser (1769) transformed steam into a universal power source.
Applications: Pumping water from coal mines (deeper coal = more fuel = more steam), driving cotton mills, powering iron forges.
AP note: Watt didn't invent the steam engine — he improved it. This distinction matters for essay evidence.
HIGH FREQ
1760s–1820s · TEXTILES
TEXTILE REVOLUTION ⚡ AP

Mechanized textile production was the first sector fully transformed by industrialization — and it created the factory system, the urban working class, and the first labor conflicts of the industrial era.

Spinning Jenny (Hargreaves, 1764): Spun 8 threads simultaneously — multiplied productivity, made cottage industry obsolete.
Water Frame (Arkwright, 1769): Power-driven spinning — required factories near rivers, concentrating workers under one roof for the first time.
Power Loom (Cartwright, 1785): Mechanized weaving destroyed the livelihood of skilled handloom weavers — sparking the Luddite movement.
Cotton gin (Whitney, 1793): Separated cotton fiber from seed 50x faster — massively increased demand for enslaved labor in the American South.
HIGH FREQ
1820s–1850s · TRANSPORT
RAILWAYS & STEAMSHIPS ⚡ AP

Railways shrank the world — moving goods overland at 30 mph instead of 4 mph. They opened interior regions to industry, created new labor markets, and consumed so much iron and coal that they amplified every other industrial sector.

Stephenson's Rocket (1829): First practical steam locomotive. The Liverpool-Manchester Railway (1830) proved railways were commercially viable.
Railway mania (1840s): 5,000+ miles of track laid in Britain in a decade. Opened coal fields, moved manufactured goods, and created the first national labor market.
Steamships: Replaced sailing ships for reliable ocean transport by 1870s. Reduced transatlantic crossings from 6 weeks to 10 days.
1837–1856 · COMMUNICATIONS & STEEL
TELEGRAPH & BESSEMER PROCESS

Two technologies that defined the Second Industrial Revolution — instantaneous global communication and cheap structural steel — transformed commerce, construction, and military power.

Electric Telegraph (1837): Morse code allowed instant long-distance communication. Transformed commerce, military coordination, and news distribution. Transatlantic cable completed 1866.
Bessemer Process (1856): Henry Bessemer's method produced cheap steel from iron in minutes instead of days. Enabled skyscrapers, longer railways, and modern warships.
Second Industrial Revolution: Steel + electricity + chemicals (1870–1920) shifted leadership from Britain to Germany and the USA.
⚡ APMED FREQ
Unit 6 · Global Spread

INDUSTRIALIZATION SPREADS

EUROPE · UNITED STATES · JAPAN · DEINDUSTRIALIZATION

How Industrialization Spread — and Where It Didn't
1820s–1870s · CONTINENTAL EUROPE
BELGIUM, FRANCE & GERMANY

Continental Europe industrialized 30–50 years after Britain, largely by copying British technology and adapting British institutional models to different political contexts.

Belgium (1820s): First Continental nation to industrialize. Copied British textile and iron technology. Located between coal fields and major rivers.
France: State-directed industrialization. Napoleon III's public works (railways, sewers) modernized Paris and accelerated industrial growth.
Germany (Prussia): Zollverein (customs union, 1834) unified German markets. After 1871 unification, Germany's state-directed industrialization overtook Britain in steel and chemicals by 1900.
⚡ APMED FREQ
1820s–1900s · UNITED STATES
AMERICAN INDUSTRIALIZATION

The United States industrialized rapidly after the Civil War, becoming the world's largest industrial economy by 1900 through unique advantages of scale, resources, and institutional innovation.

Interchangeable parts (Whitney): Standardized components allowed unskilled workers to assemble firearms — the foundation of mass production.
Transcontinental Railroad (1869): Linked Atlantic and Pacific coasts, opening vast interior resources and markets.
Robber Barons: Carnegie (steel), Rockefeller (oil), Morgan (finance) built monopolistic industrial empires using vertical and horizontal integration.
By 1900: U.S. produced more steel, coal, and manufactured goods than Britain and Germany combined.
1868 · JAPAN
MEIJI RESTORATION ⚡ AP

Japan's industrialization was uniquely state-directed and self-consciously nationalist — adopting Western technology to resist Western colonization. The only non-Western nation to fully industrialize in the 19th century.

Trigger: Perry's Black Ships (1853) forced open Japan's ports — showing the military consequences of remaining pre-industrial.
Meiji government (1868): Abolished feudal system. Sent 10,000+ students abroad. Hired foreign advisors. Built state-owned factories, railways, and shipyards.
Fukoku Kyohei: "Rich country, strong army" — industrialization explicitly linked to military power.
Result: Japan defeated Russia (1905) — first Asian nation to defeat a European power in the modern era. Validated defensive modernization as viable.
HIGH FREQ
COLONIALISM · Deliberate Underdevelopment
DEINDUSTRIALIZATION ⚡ AP

Colonialism didn't just fail to spread industrialization — it actively destroyed existing industries in colonial regions, turning them into raw material suppliers for European factories.

India: British EIC flooded India with cheap cotton textiles — Indian weavers who earned 8 annas/day earned 1–2 by 1840. India became a raw cotton supplier, not a manufacturer.
China: Opium Wars (1839, 1856) forced unequal treaties and open markets. Qing attempts at self-strengthening industrialization (1861–1895) were too limited and too late.
Ottoman Empire: Tanzimat Reforms (1839–1876) attempted modernization but accumulated European debt constrained industrialization — the "sick man of Europe."
Latin America: Export economies (guano, rubber, coffee) enriched elites but created dependency on European capital — no domestic manufacturing base.
HIGH FREQ
Unit 6 · Labor & Society

LABOR & SOCIETY

WORKING CONDITIONS · LUDDITES · UNIONS · CHILD LABOR · URBANIZATION

The Human Cost of Industrialization
CONDITIONS · Factory Life
WORKING CONDITIONS

The factory system created entirely new forms of labor exploitation — long hours, dangerous machinery, child labor, and no job security characterized the early industrial era.

Hours: 12–16 hour workdays were standard. Six-day weeks. No paid holidays or sick leave.
Child labor: Children as young as 5 worked in coal mines and textile factories. Cheap, small hands useful for machine maintenance.
Dangers: No safety regulations. Textile mills produced lung-damaging cotton dust. Coal mines collapsed. Steam boilers exploded.
First reforms: Factory Act of 1833 limited child labor to age 9 minimum, 8 hours/day maximum for children under 13 — the first regulatory intervention.
⚡ APHIGH FREQ
RESISTANCE · 1811–1816
LUDDITES ⚡ AP

The Luddites were skilled textile workers who destroyed machinery that threatened their livelihoods — one of history's most misunderstood movements. They weren't anti-technology: they opposed the wage-cutting and deskilling machinery enabled.

Who they were: Skilled framework knitters and handloom weavers whose specialized knowledge was being rendered worthless by power looms.
Ned Ludd: Mythical figure who smashed a stocking frame c. 1779. Luddites invoked his name as a symbol of worker resistance.
Response: Parliament made machine-breaking a capital offense (1812). Army deployed. Fourteen Luddites hanged in 1813.
MED FREQ
ORGANIZATION · 1820s–1900
LABOR UNIONS ⚡ AP

Workers gradually won the right to organize collectively — moving from illegal conspiracies to recognized institutions that reshaped industrial capitalism from within.

Combination Acts (1799): Banned workers' organizations. Any collective bargaining was a criminal conspiracy.
Repeal (1824): Workers gained the right to organize. Trade unions grew rapidly in skilled trades — miners, railway workers, printers.
Gains by 1900: 10-hour workday (1847), weekend, holiday pay, safety regulations — all won through collective action.
AP thesis: Unions were effective at reforming capitalism from within — not replacing it, as Marx predicted.
HIGH FREQ
URBANIZATION · New Cities
THE URBAN TRANSFORMATION

Industrialization created the modern city — pulling millions off farms and into urban industrial centers that offered wages but also overcrowding, disease, and social dislocation.

Manchester: Population grew from 25,000 (1772) to 300,000 (1850). Friedrich Engels documented appalling conditions in The Condition of the Working Class in England (1845).
Disease: Cholera epidemics (1831, 1848) killed thousands in overcrowded cities before germ theory and public sanitation were established.
Social mobility: Cities also created new middle classes — factory managers, lawyers, accountants — who benefited enormously from industrialization.
Unit 6 · Economic Ideas

CAPITALISM & ITS CRITICS

ADAM SMITH · LAISSEZ-FAIRE · MARX · SOCIALISM · CLASS CONFLICT

Primary Source · Communist Manifesto, 1848
"The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles. Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman — in a word, oppressor and oppressed."
— Karl Marx & Friedrich Engels, The Communist Manifesto, 1848
The Battle of Economic Ideas
LIBERAL ECONOMICS · Adam Smith, 1776
LAISSEZ-FAIRE CAPITALISM ⚡ AP

Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations (1776) argued that free markets, guided by the "invisible hand" of self-interest, produce better outcomes than government direction — and justified industrial capitalism's lack of regulation.

Invisible hand: Self-interested actors competing in free markets unintentionally benefit society — no government planning required.
Division of labor: Specialization dramatically increases productivity. Smith's pin factory example: 10 workers making pins each doing one step produce 48,000/day vs 10 each making complete pins.
Free trade: Britain repealed the Corn Laws (1846) — accepting cheaper foreign grain to keep wages down and sell manufactured goods globally.
Social Darwinism: Later thinkers (Spencer) applied Darwin's "survival of the fittest" to economics — justifying poverty as natural selection for the unfit.
HIGH FREQ
MARXISM · Karl Marx, 1848
CLASS STRUGGLE & COMMUNISM ⚡ AP

Karl Marx argued that industrial capitalism exploited workers by extracting surplus value from their labor — and that this class struggle would inevitably produce revolution and a classless communist society.

Bourgeoisie vs Proletariat: Factory owners (bourgeoisie) exploit workers (proletariat) by paying wages below the value workers produce — extracting "surplus value" as profit.
Historical materialism: History is driven by class struggle — each era's dominant class sows the seeds of its own overthrow.
Communist Manifesto (1848): "Workers of the world, unite! You have nothing to lose but your chains." Published during the Revolutions of 1848.
AP note: Marx's prediction didn't materialize in advanced industrial nations — revolutions came in Russia and China, not Britain or Germany.
HIGH FREQ
UTOPIAN & REVISIONIST SOCIALISM
SOCIALIST ALTERNATIVES

Many thinkers tried to reform industrial capitalism without revolution — from utopian communes to parliamentary socialism to Bismarck's welfare state.

Robert Owen: British mill owner who built the model community New Lanark — fair wages, schools, no child labor. Proved humane factories could be profitable.
Revisionist socialism: Eduard Bernstein argued workers could achieve gains through parliamentary democracy — no revolution needed.
Bismarck's welfare state (1880s): Introduced state pensions, accident insurance, and sick pay — deliberately to undercut socialist movements by giving workers a stake in the existing system.
⚡ APMED FREQ
WOMEN · New Opportunities & Limits
WOMEN IN INDUSTRIAL SOCIETY

Industrialization created new economic roles for women — but also new forms of exploitation. The Cult of Domesticity restricted middle-class women to the home while working-class women labored in factories.

Factory labor: Women and children made up 75% of early textile workers — paid half of men's wages for the same work.
Cult of Domesticity: Middle-class ideology that women's place was in the home — "separate spheres." Actually confined to women who could afford not to work.
Suffrage movement: Seneca Falls Convention (1848) demanded voting rights. British suffragettes became increasingly militant by 1900. Women gained vote in most Western nations by 1920.
⚡ APMED FREQ
Unit 6 · c. 1870–1914

IMPERIALISM

SCRAMBLE FOR AFRICA · BRITISH INDIA · CHINA · RESISTANCE MOVEMENTS

Primary Source · Cecil Rhodes, c. 1895
"The annexation of territory is a duty — we must fill every space we can to prevent others from filling it."
— Cecil Rhodes, British imperialist and founder of Rhodesia, c. 1895
The New Imperialism (1870s–1914)
EXPLANATION · Why Now?
MOTIVES FOR EMPIRE ⚡ AP

The "New Imperialism" of the late 19th century was qualitatively different from earlier colonialism — driven by industrial capitalism's specific needs. Factories required raw materials; manufacturers needed markets; investors sought returns; navies needed coaling stations. Ideology justified what economics demanded.

Economic: Raw materials (rubber, cotton, metals, oil) for factories; captive markets for manufactured goods; investment opportunities for surplus capital.
Strategic: Naval coaling stations for global power projection. Suez Canal (1869) made control of Egypt critical for Britain's route to India.
Social Darwinism: Applied Darwin's "survival of the fittest" to nations and "races" — argued European domination was natural, scientific, inevitable.
White Man's Burden: Kipling (1899) framed empire as a civilizing mission — bringing Christianity and "civilization" to peoples deemed primitive. Masked economic exploitation in moral language.
HIGH FREQ
1884–1914 · Africa
SCRAMBLE FOR AFRICA ⚡ AP

The Berlin Conference (1884–85) gathered 14 European nations to divide Africa with no African representatives present. Powers drew straight-line borders cutting across ethnic and linguistic groups — creating artificial states whose borders still cause conflict today.

Berlin Conference (1884): "Effective occupation" rule — powers had to actually control territory to claim it. Triggered a land grab across the continent.
By 1914: Only Ethiopia and Liberia remained independent. Britain controlled 30% of Africa's population; France held the largest territory by area.
Congo Free State: King Leopold II's personal colony. Forced rubber extraction killed an estimated 10 million Congolese — one of history's worst atrocities. Eventually transferred to Belgian state after international outcry.
Artificial borders: European straight-line borders split ethnic groups and lumped enemies together — the root cause of many post-independence conflicts in Africa.
HIGH FREQ
1757–1947 · South Asia
BRITISH INDIA — THE RAJ ⚡ AP

Britain's most prized colonial possession — the "jewel in the crown." The East India Company ruled India until the Sepoy Mutiny (1857) triggered direct Crown rule. India supplied raw materials and was systematically deindustrialized to serve British manufacturing interests.

Sepoy Mutiny (1857): Indian soldiers in British service rebelled over rumors of pork/beef-greased cartridges — violating Hindu and Muslim religious laws. After brutal suppression, Britain dissolved the EIC and established the British Raj.
Economic exploitation: India supplied raw cotton, tea, indigo, and opium. 40,000 miles of railroad built — for resource extraction to ports, not Indian internal development.
Famines: 12–29 million Indians died in famines 1876–1900, partly because food was exported during shortages. Britain prioritized export revenue over Indian lives.
Indian National Congress (1885): First organized political movement for Indian self-rule — laid the foundation for Gandhi's independence movement in the 20th century.
HIGH FREQ
RESISTANCE · Global
ANTI-COLONIAL RESISTANCE ⚡ AP

Imperial conquest was never uncontested. Resistance took every form — from military battles and uprisings to political movements, economic boycotts, and cultural preservation. These movements laid the groundwork for 20th-century decolonization.

Battle of Adwa (1896): Ethiopia's Emperor Menelik II decisively defeated Italy — the only African military victory against a colonial army in the Scramble era. Ethiopia remained independent throughout the colonial period.
Boxer Rebellion (1899–1901): Chinese nationalists ("Righteous Fists") attacked foreign presence and Christian missionaries. Suppressed by 8-nation military coalition — further weakening the Qing.
Indian National Congress: Founded 1885 by Western-educated Indians — initially sought reform within British system, later demanded independence. Political resistance, not military.
Cultural resistance: Language preservation, syncretic religion, and local cultural practices survived colonial suppression — laying the foundation for nationalist identity movements.
HIGH FREQ
Unit 6 · c. 1712–1914

KEY EVENTS TIMELINE

1712–1914 — FROM NEWCOMEN'S ENGINE TO THE EVE OF WWI

1712
Newcomen's Steam Engine
Thomas Newcomen builds the first practical steam engine for pumping water from coal mines. Inefficient but proof of concept — Watt will transform it 57 years later.
1764
Spinning Jenny
James Hargreaves invents the spinning jenny — spins 8 threads simultaneously. First major mechanization of textile production. Threatens cottage industry workers.
1769
Watt's Separate Condenser
James Watt's separate condenser makes steam power 4x more efficient. Now viable for factories and pumping mines. The pivotal moment of the Industrial Revolution.
1776
Wealth of Nations Published
Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations provides the intellectual framework for industrial capitalism — free markets, division of labor, and laissez-faire economics.
1785
Power Loom
Edmund Cartwright's power loom mechanizes weaving — destroying the livelihood of skilled handloom weavers and sparking the first organized worker resistance.
1799
Combination Acts
British Parliament bans workers' organizations. Any collective bargaining becomes criminal conspiracy. The legal foundation of laissez-faire labor policy.
1811
Luddite Movement Begins
Skilled textile workers smash machinery across Northern England. Parliament makes machine-breaking a capital offense. 14 Luddites hanged at York, 1813.
1824
Combination Acts Repealed
Workers gain the legal right to organize. Trade unions begin growing in skilled trades — laying the foundation for collective bargaining and eventual labor reform.
1829
Stephenson's Rocket
George Stephenson's locomotive wins the Rainhill Trials. The Liverpool-Manchester Railway opens 1830 — the world's first intercity steam railway.
1848
Communist Manifesto
Marx and Engels publish The Communist Manifesto — a direct response to industrial capitalism's exploitation. Published during the Revolutions of 1848, initially ignored. Influential after 1917.
1856
Bessemer Process
Henry Bessemer's steelmaking process produces cheap structural steel — enabling skyscrapers, longer railways, and steel warships. Triggers the Second Industrial Revolution.
1868
Meiji Restoration
Japan's Meiji government launches state-directed industrialization to resist Western colonization. Japan defeats Russia in 1905 — validating defensive modernization.
1880s
Bismarck's Welfare State
Germany introduces state pensions, accident insurance, and sick pay — the world's first welfare state. Designed to undercut socialist movements by giving workers a stake in the system.
1857
Sepoy Mutiny — British Raj Begins ⚡ AP
Indian soldiers in British service revolt over greased cartridges violating Hindu and Muslim religious laws. Brutally suppressed. Britain dissolves the East India Company and establishes direct Crown rule — the British Raj.
1869
Suez Canal Opens
French-built canal connects Mediterranean to Red Sea — cutting the Britain-India voyage from months to weeks. Britain purchases controlling shares in 1875. Control of the canal becomes the central justification for British occupation of Egypt (1882).
1884–85
Berlin Conference — Scramble for Africa ⚡ AP
14 European nations divide Africa with no African representatives present. "Effective occupation" rule triggers land grab. By 1914 only Ethiopia and Liberia remain independent. Artificial borders drawn then still cause conflicts today.
1896
Battle of Adwa — Ethiopia Defeats Italy ⚡ AP
Emperor Menelik II defeats the Italian army at Adwa — the only decisive African military victory against a European colonial force during the Scramble era. Ethiopia remains independent throughout the colonial period.
1899–1901
Boxer Rebellion — China
Chinese nationalist "Righteous Fists" attack foreign presence and Christian missionaries. 8-nation coalition suppresses the rebellion. Further weakens the Qing dynasty and increases foreign sphere-of-influence control of Chinese territory.
1913
Ford's Moving Assembly Line
Henry Ford introduces the first moving assembly line at Highland Park, Michigan. Model T production time drops from 12+ hours to 93 minutes. Mass production era begins — and so does the Second Industrial Revolution's full maturity.
